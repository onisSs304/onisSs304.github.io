A Survey on Universal Approximation Theorem: View from the Depth

Introduction

In this post, I want to discover the Universal Approximation Theorem, a foundational result in approximation theory and deep learning. Broadly speaking, this theorem states that even relatively simple feedforward neural networks, under mild assumptions on the activation function, can approximate any continuous function on a compact domain to arbitrary accuracy. This result provides a theoretical justification for the expressive power of neural networks and explains why they are capable of modeling highly complex nonlinear relationships.

We will only focus on the expressive power of feedforward neural networks. Additionally, it can be considered as an extension of my latest research project on Deep Learning Theory and Optimization. Furthermore, I only consider networks with one hidden layer and arbitrary width.

General Results

We begin with the most well-known result:

Theorem 1 [Cybenko, 1989]
Let \( \sigma \) be any continuous discriminatory function, meaning that for any measure \( \mu \) such that \[ \int \sigma(w^T x + \theta)\, d\mu(x) = 0, \quad \forall\, w \in \mathbb{R}^d,\, \theta \in \mathbb{R} \implies \mu = 0, \] the set of functions \[ x \in [0,1]^d \mapsto \sum_{i=1}^N v_i \sigma(w_i^T x + b_i) \] where \( N \in \mathbb{N},\, w_i \in \mathbb{R}^d,\, b_i \in \mathbb{R} \), is dense in \( C([0,1]^d, \mathbb{R}) \).

One may feel more familiar with the assumption that \( \sigma \) is a sigmoidal function, i.e. \( \sigma(x) \xrightarrow{x \to -\infty} 0 \) and \( \sigma(x) \xrightarrow{x \to +\infty} 1 \). A sigmoidal function is also discriminatory, so the theorem still holds. Roughly speaking, for any continuous function \( f \) and any \( \epsilon > 0 \), there exist \( N \in \mathbb{N},\, (w_i),\, (v_i),\, (b_i) \) such that: \[ \left| f(x) - \sum_{i=1}^N v_i \sigma(w_i^T x + b_i) \right| \leq \epsilon, \quad \forall\, x \in [0,1]^d \]

Many subsequent works pushed in this direction: [Hornik, 1991] on approximating \( L^p \) functions, and [Funahashi, 1989] on approximation by bounded, monotone increasing continuous activation functions. Based on these results, the most general version of the Universal Approximation Theorem was proven by Leshno, Lin, Pinkus, and Schocken in 1993:

Theorem 2 [Leshno et al., 1993]
Let \( \sigma \) be a locally bounded piecewise continuous function. Then the set of functions \[ x \in [0,1]^d \mapsto \sum_{i=1}^N v_i \sigma(w_i^T x + b_i) \] is dense in \( C([0,1]^d, \mathbb{R}) \) if and only if \( \sigma \) is not a polynomial. Therefore, popular activation functions such as ReLU, tanh, sigmoid, etc. all possess universal approximation capacity.

The breakthrough of this theorem is that it unifies all previous results into a single necessary and sufficient condition on the activation function. Furthermore, dropping the continuity assumption does not significantly affect the universal approximation property.

Fixed Weights

Until now, we have discussed neural networks with arbitrary weights. We now analyze the situation where the weights are fixed, examining whether neural nets can still serve as universal approximators. When all weights are fixed, the approximation capacity is significantly limited.

In general, it seems impossible to approximate an arbitrary continuous function over \( \mathbb{R} \) by a neural network with sigmoidal activation and fixed weights. For example, \( f(x) = x \) satisfies \( \lim_{x \to +\infty} f(x) = +\infty \), so it cannot be approximated uniformly. We need to either restrict to a closed interval or add assumptions on \( f \) at infinity. A result of Hahm and Hong addresses this:

Theorem 3 [Hahm & Hong, 2004]
Let \( f \) be a continuous function on a closed interval of \( \mathbb{R} \) such that \( \lim_{|x| \to \infty} f(x) = 0 \). If \( \sigma \) is a bounded, measurable sigmoidal function, then for sufficiently small \( \epsilon \), there exist constants \( v_i, b_i \) and positive integers \( K, N \) such that: \[ \left| f(x) - \sum_{i=1}^N v_i \sigma(Kx + b_i) \right| < \epsilon, \quad x \in \mathbb{R} \]
Remark. This result generalizes to approximation of continuous functions on a compact set with a similar conclusion.

From the proof, the weights depend strictly on the functional properties of the activation function. For instance, if \( \sigma \) is the Heaviside function \( \sigma(x) = \mathbf{1}_{x \geq 0}(x) \), the weight \( K \) can be any positive number. In contrast, for \( \sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}} \), \( K \) must be sufficiently large.

Remark. [Xu, 2013] used even, \( 2\pi \)-periodic continuous activation functions to approximate \( 2\pi \)-periodic continuous functions — and hence any continuous function on \( [-\pi, \pi] \).

Limitation of Fixed-Weight Networks

All results discussed so far apply only to univariate functions. The results of [Hahm & Hong, 2004] and [Xu, 2013] do not extend to multivariate functions. Consider a one-hidden-layer network with fixed weights: \[ h(x) = \sum_{i=1}^r c_i \sigma(\langle w_i, x \rangle + b_i) \] This is a ridge function — a function of \( \langle w_i, x \rangle \), not of \( x \) directly. The set \( \mathbf{H} \) of all such functions is not dense in \( C(Q, \mathbb{R}) \) for a compact \( Q \subset \mathbb{R}^d \). Hence approximation of all continuous multivariate functions is impossible, as shown in Theorem 5.1 of [Lin & Pinkus, 1993].

Random Weights and Biases

This section covers the part I wanted to explore in my project but could not due to time constraints. The intuition: let \( N \) be the number of neurons; in each neuron, we randomly choose the weight and bias. The only remaining parameter to learn is the output weight in the linear layer.

The most impressive approach comes from the Random Vector Functional Link (RVFL) network. For any \( f \in C([a,b]^d, \mathbb{R}) \), there exists a limit-integral representation: \[ f(x) = \lim_{L \to a} \int_V T[f(\lambda)] G_{\lambda, L}(x)\, d\lambda \] Approximating this via Monte Carlo sampling with \( (\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n) \) i.i.d. uniform on \( V \): \[ f(x) \approx \frac{|V|}{n} \sum_{k=1}^n T[f(\lambda_k)] G_{\lambda_k, l}(x) = \sum_{k=1}^n a_k G_{\lambda_k, l}(x) \] If \( G_{\lambda, l} \) is regarded as an activation function, we have approximated \( f \) by a single hidden-layer network with random weights and biases: \[ f_n(x) = \sum_{k=1}^n a_k g(w_k x + b_k) \]

Rigorous Theory

Theorem 4 [Igelnik & Pao, 1995]
Let \( f_n(x) = \sum_{k=1}^n a_k g(w_k x + b_k) \) where \( \lambda_n = (w_1, \ldots, w_n, b_1, \ldots, b_n) \) follows some probability measure. For any compact \( K \subset [0,1]^d \) and any absolutely integrable activation function \( g \), there exists a sequence of RVFL networks \( (f_n)_n \) and probability measures \( \mu_n \) such that: \[ \lim_{n \to \infty} \mathbb{E}\left[ \int_K (f(x) - f_n(x))^2\, dx \right] = 0 \]
Remark 1. The probability measure for \( \lambda_n \) is specified in the original paper. The remaining parameters lie in the linear layer — in learning theory terms, we only need to learn the linear coefficients, while weights and biases are drawn from a probability measure.
Remark 2. The distribution for weights and biases must be continuous. A discrete distribution leads to the same limitations as fixed-weight networks.

Understanding this, [Huang et al., 2006] introduced a new gradient algorithm that works well in random networks and overcomes the memory usage of backpropagation. For a more mathematical treatment using functional analysis, [Neufeld & Schmocker, 2023] extends these results to \( L^p \) function approximation with non-polynomial activation functions.

Random Weights and Learned Biases

A recent work [Lajoie & Mazzucato, 2025] addresses the large neuron count required by fully random networks, proving universal approximation with random weights and learnable biases under hard constraints on the activation function.

Definition
An activation function \( \sigma \) is a \( \gamma \)-bounding activation if it allows universal approximation even when individual parameters are bounded by \( \gamma \). ReLU and the Heaviside step function are \( \gamma \)-parameter bounding for any \( \gamma > 0 \). Furthermore, \( \sigma \) is a bias-learning activation if it is continuous and there exists \( r \in \mathbb{R} \) such that \( \sigma(x) = 0 \) for all \( x < r \).

Let \( p_R \) denote the uniform distribution on \( [-R, R] \) with \( R > \gamma \). The main result is:

Theorem 5 [Lajoie & Mazzucato, 2025]
Assume \( \sigma \) is \( \gamma \)-bias-learning and \( h : U \to \mathbb{R}^l \) is continuous on a compact \( U \subset \mathbb{R}^d \). Then for any \( \varepsilon > 0 \) and \( \delta \in (0,1) \), there exist a hidden-layer width \( m \in \mathbb{N} \) and bias vector \( b \in \mathbb{R}^m \) such that, with probability at least \( 1 - \delta \), a neural network with each weight sampled from \( p_R \) approximates \( h \) with error less than \( \varepsilon \).
Remark 3. A natural open question: does this theorem hold for bounded sigmoidal activations, which are among the most popular and widely used in learning tasks?
Conclusion

Together, we have surveyed one of the most fundamental theorems in machine learning across several settings: the general case, fixed weights, and random weights. While perhaps less immediately applicable, these results deepen our understanding of why neural networks work. In practice, networks with arbitrary depth and bounded width are often preferred for learning problems.

Beyond the works highlighted here, many other magnificent papers have studied the approximation capacities of neural networks. Since this is a small personal survey, I cannot cover all of them.
