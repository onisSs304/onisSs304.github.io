momentum: acceleration for optimization algorithms

Introduction

I have never taken seriously the definition and intuition of momentum for optimization — I have just used it in practice without understanding why it works. So today, I want to revisit the origin as well as the motivation for this method in the original work of [Polyak, 1962].

There are many interpretations on the Internet that this technique is like a ball rolling down a hill that gathers enough momentum to overcome a plateau region and reach a global minimum instead of getting stuck at a local minimum. But I still did not fully grasp this intuition, so I want to use this post to clarify my understanding.

Existence and Uniqueness of Minimizers

Let us make assumptions for a function \( f : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R} \) that is coercive and continuous. Such a function admits at least one global minimizer. Alternatively, if \( f \) is continuous on a non-empty compact set, it also admits a global minimizer. With the additional assumption of strong convexity, the global minimizer is unique. My objective here is to set up a regime in which the function has at least one global minimizer for further analysis.

Let \( x \) be a global minimum of \( f \) on a compact set \( \mathcal{X} \subset \mathbb{R}^d \). If \( f \) is differentiable, then \( \nabla f(x) = 0 \), or \( 0 \in \partial f(x) \) in the non-differentiable case. Indeed, suppose \( \nabla f(x) \neq 0 \) and let \( \Delta x > 0 \) be sufficiently small: \[ f(x + \Delta x) - f(x) = \langle \nabla f(x), \Delta x \rangle + O(\|\Delta x\|^2) \] When \( \Delta x \neq 0 \) and \( \|\Delta x\| \to 0 \), obviously \( f(x + \Delta x) - f(x) > 0 \) and \( O(\|\Delta x\|^2) \to 0 \). Setting \( \Delta x = -\nabla f(x)/n \): \[ \langle \nabla f(x), \Delta x \rangle = -\|\nabla f(x)\|^2 / n \] Therefore \( \langle \nabla f(x), \Delta x \rangle + O(\|\Delta x\|^2) < 0 \) for \( n \) sufficiently large, while the left-hand side is always positive — a contradiction. So we must have \( \nabla f(x) = 0 \). The same analysis can be carried out in the non-differentiable case. Let \( \partial f(x) \) be the subdifferential of \( f \) at \( x \): \[ \forall\, g \in \partial f(x): f(x) \geq f(y) + \langle g, x - y \rangle \] then \( x \) is the global minimizer of \( f \) iff \( 0 \in \partial f(x) \).

Conclusion. If \( x \) is the global minimizer of \( f \), then \( \nabla f(x) = 0 \). Therefore, a reasonable way to find global minima is to solve \( \nabla f(x) = 0 \). However, the roots of this equation are not always global minima — they may be local minima or saddle points. To ensure a global minimum, one may need second-order conditions, but we will not discuss these here. We focus on iterative methods to find roots of \( \nabla f(x) = 0 \).
Iterative Methods to Solve an Equation

Let us consider a general equation \( P(x) = 0 \). When we talk about iterative methods, we think about sequences of the form: \[ x_n = \alpha_k x_{n-1} + \cdots + \alpha_1 x_{n-k} \] Let \( X^n = (x^n, x^{n+1}, \ldots, x^{n+k-1})^T \). Then \( X^{n+1} = AX^n \) where: \[ A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 \\ \alpha_1 & \alpha_2 & \cdots & \alpha_k \end{pmatrix} \] Let us simplify by considering only the case \( k = 2 \). Let \( x^\star \) be the root of \( P(x) \). We need to find a sequence converging to \( x^\star \): \[ x_{n+2} - x^\star = \alpha_2(x_{n+1} - x^\star) + \alpha_1(x_n - x^\star) + x^\star(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2 - 1), \quad x_0, x_1 \in \mathbb{R} \] If \( \alpha_1 + \alpha_2 \neq 1 \), then \( x_n - x^\star \) never converges to 0. So we need \( \alpha_1 + \alpha_2 = 1 \). However, in this case: \[ x_n - x^\star = a + b\rho^n, \quad \forall\, n \] and \( x_n \) converges to \( x^\star \) iff \( a = 0 \) and \( |\rho| < 1 \), which is initialization-dependent. We would need another method since we have no prior knowledge of \( x^\star \).

In [Polyak, 1962], a nonlinear term related to \( P \) is added to control the convergence and the limit of the sequence. By Taylor expansion analysis: \[ P(x) = P(x^\star) + P'(x^\star)(x - x^\star) + o(|x - x^\star|) = P'(x^\star)(x - x^\star) + o(|x - x^\star|) \] The new sequence is: \begin{align*} x_{n+2} - x^\star &= \alpha_2(x_{n+1} - x^\star) + \alpha_1(x_n - x^\star) + \beta_2 P(x_{n+1}) + \beta_1 P(x_n) \\ &= (x_{n+1} - x^\star)(\alpha_2 + P'(x^\star)\beta_2) + (x_n - x^\star)(\alpha_1 + P'(x^\star)\beta_1) \\ &\quad + o(|x_n - x^\star| + |x_{n+1} - x^\star|) \end{align*} Define \( y_n = x_n - x^\star \), \( X_n = (x_n, x_{n+1})^T \), \( \gamma_2 = \alpha_2 + P'(x^\star)\beta_2 \), \( \gamma_1 = \alpha_1 + P'(x^\star)\beta_1 \). So \( X_{n+1} = AX_n + Y(X_n) \) where \( Y(X) \in o(\|x_n - x^\star\| + \|x_{n+1} - x^\star\|) \). Since \( \alpha_1 + \alpha_2 = 1 \), the parameters to tune are \( \alpha_1, \beta_1, \beta_2 \). Whatever the value of \( P'(x^\star) \), we can tune these parameters so that the eigenvalues of \( A \) both lie in \( (-1, 1) \). From [Polyak, 1962]:

Lemma
Suppose that the linear operator \( T \) satisfies: \[ \|T^n\| \leq C(\varepsilon)(q + \varepsilon)^n, \quad q < 1 \] and \( \|Y(X)\| = o(\|X\|) \). Then the series \( X^{n+1} = TX^n + Y(X^n) \) approaches zero with sufficiently small \( X^0 \), and \( \|X^n\| \leq C'(\varepsilon)\|X^0\|(q + \varepsilon)^n \).
Proof

Let \( \varepsilon > 0 \) be chosen sufficiently small so that \( \rho = q + \varepsilon < 1 \). The operator bound becomes \( \|T^n\| \leq C\rho^n \) where \( C = C(\varepsilon) \).

By the definition of little-\( o \), for any \( \delta > 0 \) there exists \( r > 0 \) such that if \( \|X\| \leq r \), then \( \|Y(X)\| \leq \delta\|X\| \).

Unrolling the recurrence \( X^{n+1} = TX^n + Y(X^n) \):

\[ X^n = T^n X^0 + \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} T^{n-1-k} Y(X^k) \]

Taking norms and applying the triangle inequality:

\[ \|X^n\| \leq \|T^n\|\|X^0\| + \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \|T^{n-1-k}\|\|Y(X^k)\| \leq C\rho^n \|X^0\| + \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} C\rho^{n-1-k}\|Y(X^k)\| \]

We proceed by induction with hypothesis \( \|X^k\| \leq C'\rho_1^k\|X^0\| \) for some \( \rho < \rho_1 < 1 \) and \( C' > C \). Substituting into the inequality:

\begin{align*} \|X^n\| &\leq C\rho^n \|X^0\| + CC'\delta\|X^0\|\rho^{n-1} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\left(\frac{\rho_1}{\rho}\right)^k \\ &\leq C\rho^n \|X^0\| + \frac{CC'\delta}{\rho_1 - \rho}\rho_1^n \|X^0\| \leq \left(C + \frac{CC'\delta}{\rho_1 - \rho}\right)\rho_1^n \|X^0\| \end{align*}

The induction is complete when:

\[ \delta \leq \frac{(C' - C)(\rho_1 - \rho)}{CC'} \]

Since \( \|Y(X)\| = o(\|X\|) \), we can choose \( r \) small enough to satisfy this condition. Therefore \( \|X^n\| \leq C'\rho_1^n \|X^0\| \to 0 \) as \( n \to \infty \).

□

In our case, \( TX = AX \) is matrix-vector multiplication and \( A \) has eigenvalues in \( (-1, 1) \), which satisfies the assumptions of the lemma. This concludes the convergence of the sequence above.

Conclusion. By adding the term \( P(x) \), we force the limit of \( (x_n)_n \) to necessarily be a root of \( P \).
Connection to optimization. Setting \( \beta_1 = 0 \), \( \beta = -\beta_2 \neq 0 \), \( \alpha = -\alpha_1 \), and letting \( P \) be the gradient of a function to be optimized, we get: \[ x_{n+1} = (1 + \alpha)x_n - \alpha x_{n-1} - \beta P(x_n) \] which is exactly gradient descent with momentum in optimization.
Convergence of Momentum Methods

In the last section, we used the intuition from iterative sequences to derive the formulation of momentum. One may generalize to multi-step methods, but that would make the problem more sophisticated.

Quadratic Loss Function

This is perhaps the simplest explanation of why momentum is useful in practice. Consider the convex quadratic model, which appears in many settings. If \( f \) is twice differentiable, by Taylor expansion: \[ f(x + \Delta x) = f(x) + \langle \nabla f(x), \Delta x \rangle + \Delta x^T H(x)\Delta x + o(\|\Delta x\|^2) \] where \( H(x) \) is the Hessian matrix (symmetric, invertible, and positive semi-definite). Dropping higher-order terms and setting \( A = H(x) \), \( b = -\nabla f(x) \), \( w = \Delta x \), the solution is \( w^\star = A^{-1}b \). The gradient is \( \nabla F(w) = Aw - b \), and GD and GD with momentum update respectively: \[ w_{n+1} = w_n - \alpha(Aw_n - b), \quad w_{n+1} = w_n(1 + \beta) - \beta w_{n-1} - \alpha(Aw_n - b) \] The analysis for these two methods is fully described in [Goh, 2017]. Briefly, with optimal parameters and condition number \( k \) of \( A \), the convergence rates are: \[ \gamma_{\mathrm{GDM}} = \frac{\sqrt{k} - 1}{\sqrt{k} + 1} \quad (\text{Momentum}), \qquad \gamma_{\mathrm{GD}} = \frac{k - 1}{k + 1} \quad (\text{GD}) \] Since \( k > 1 \) and \( \sqrt{k} < k \), we have \( \gamma_{\mathrm{GDM}} < \gamma_{\mathrm{GD}} \). In particular, when the problem is ill-conditioned (very large condition number), GD with momentum is highly more effective than GD.

Convergence for Strongly Convex Optimization

In the last subsection, we discussed the convergence rate via the structure of the Hessian. However, we do not always have access to Hessian information. We now extend our analysis to a broader class of functions.

\( \mu \)-strong convexity. A function \( f : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R} \) is \( \mu \)-strongly convex if \( \mathrm{dom}(f) \) is convex and for all \( \lambda \in (0,1) \) and \( x, y \in \mathrm{dom}(f) \): \[ f(\lambda x + (1-\lambda)y) \leq \lambda f(x) + (1-\lambda)f(y) - \frac{1}{2}\lambda(1-\lambda)\|x-y\|^2 \] A well-known consequence for differentiable strongly convex \( f \) is: \[ \forall\, x, y \in \mathrm{dom}(f): f(x) \geq f(y) + \langle \nabla f(y), x-y \rangle + \frac{\mu}{2}\|x-y\|^2 \]

\( L \)-smoothness. A differentiable function \( f \) is \( L \)-smooth if its gradient is \( L \)-Lipschitz: \[ \forall\, x, y \in \mathrm{dom}(f): \|\nabla f(x) - \nabla f(y)\| \leq L\|x-y\| \] which gives the quadratic upper bound: \[ f(x) \leq f(y) + \langle \nabla f(y), x-y \rangle + \frac{L}{2}\|x-y\|^2 \]

GD without momentum. For classical gradient descent \( x_{n+1} = x_n - \alpha\nabla f(x_n) \), by \( L \)-smoothness: \begin{align*} f(x_{n+1}) &\leq f(x_n) - \alpha\|\nabla f(x_n)\|^2 + \frac{L\alpha^2}{2}\|\nabla f(x_n)\|^2 = f(x_n) + \alpha\|\nabla f(x_n)\|^2\left(\frac{L\alpha}{2} - 1\right) \end{align*} We need \( 0 < \alpha < 2/L \) to ensure a descent direction. The optimal \( \alpha \) minimizing the bound is \( 1/L \). By Theorem 2.1.15 in [Nesterov, 2018]:

Theorem — Convergence of GD
If \( f \) is \( L \)-smooth and \( \mu \)-strongly convex and \( 0 < \alpha \leq \dfrac{2}{\mu + L} \), then GD generates a sequence \( \{x_k\} \) such that: \[ \|x_k - x^\star\|^2 \leq \left(1 - \frac{2\alpha\mu L}{\mu + L}\right)^k \|x_0 - x^\star\|^2 \]

GD with momentum. The first analysis for global convergence of GD with momentum is fully described in [Ghadimi et al., 2015]. I will prove the convergence result for the weighted average of iterates: \[ \frac{1}{T}\sum_{i=0}^{T-1} f(x_i) - f^\star \] Let \( z_k = x_k + \dfrac{\beta}{1-\beta}(x_k - x_{k-1}) \), so: \[ z_{k+1} = z_k - \frac{\alpha}{1-\beta}\nabla f(x_k), \quad z_0 = x_0 \] Let \( x^\star \) be a global minimizer of \( f \) and \( r_k = \|z_k - x^\star\| \). Then: \begin{align*} r_{k+1}^2 &= r_k^2 - \frac{2\alpha}{1-\beta}\langle \nabla f(x_k), x_k - x^\star \rangle - \frac{2\alpha\beta}{(1-\beta)^2}\langle \nabla f(x_k), x_k - x_{k-1} \rangle + \frac{\alpha^2}{(1-\beta)^2}\|\nabla f(x_k)\|^2 \end{align*} By convexity: \[ \langle \nabla f(x_k), x_k - x^\star \rangle \geq f(x_k) - f^\star, \quad \langle \nabla f(x_k), x_k - x_{k-1} \rangle \geq f(x_k) - f(x_{k-1}) \] Using \( L \)-smoothness: \( \|\nabla f(x_k)\|^2 \leq L\langle x_k - x^\star, \nabla f(x_k) \rangle \). Assuming \( \alpha < \dfrac{2(1-\beta)}{L} \): \begin{align*} r_{k+1}^2 \leq r_k^2 - \left(\frac{2\alpha}{1-\beta} - \frac{L\alpha^2}{(1-\beta)^2}\right)(f(x_k) - f^\star) - \frac{2\alpha\beta}{(1-\beta)^2}(f(x_k) - f(x_{k-1})) \end{align*} Summing over \( T \) iterations with \( \gamma = \dfrac{2\alpha}{1-\beta} - \dfrac{L\alpha^2}{(1-\beta)^2} > 0 \), \( \lambda = \dfrac{2\alpha\beta}{(1-\beta)^2} > 0 \), and using \( f(x_{T-1}) \geq f^\star \): \[ r_T^2 \leq r_0^2 - \gamma\sum_{i=0}^{T-1}(f(x_k) - f^\star) - \lambda(f(x_{T-1}) - f(x_0)) \] \begin{align*} &\gamma\sum_{i=0}^{T-1}(f(x_k) - f^\star) \leq r_0^2 - r_T^2 - \lambda(f(x_{T-1}) - f(x_0)) \leq r_0^2 - \lambda(f^\star - f(x_0)) \\ &\implies f(\bar{x}_T) - f^\star \leq \frac{\|x_0 - x^\star\|^2}{T\gamma} + \frac{\lambda(f(x_0) - f^\star)}{T\gamma} \end{align*} The final implication comes from the convexity of \( f \): \[ \frac{1}{T}\sum_{i=1}^{T-1} f(x_i) \leq f\!\left(\frac{1}{T}\sum_{i=1}^{T-1} x_i\right) = f(\bar{x}_T) \]

Stochastic GD with Momentum

Stochastic gradient descent exploits the robustness of first-order methods, as high precision is not always required in machine learning. The main idea is to replace the exact gradient by a random estimate called the stochastic gradient. Note that a gradient estimate is not necessarily a descent direction. Let \( f \) be the function to optimize and \( g(\theta, x) \) a stochastic gradient where \( \theta \) follows a probability distribution. We make two standard assumptions:

Assumption 1 — Unbiasedness: \( \mathbb{E}_\theta[g(\theta, x)] = \nabla f(x) \)

Assumption 2 — Bounded variance: \( \mathbb{E}_\theta[\|g(\theta, x)\|^2] < B \)

The SGD update rule is \( x_{n+1} = x_n - \alpha_n g(\theta, x_n) \).

Theorem — Convergence of SGD
Assume \( f \) is \( L \)-smooth and the two assumptions above hold. For an initial guess \( x_0 \), the SGD iterates \( x_t \) with constant step-size \( \alpha \) satisfy: \[ \frac{1}{T}\,\mathbb{E}_\theta\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\|\nabla f(x_t)\|^2\right] \leq \frac{f(x_0) - f^\star}{T\alpha} + \frac{\alpha LB}{2} \]
Proof

By \( L \)-smoothness:

\begin{align*} f(x_{n+1}) &\leq f(x_n) - \alpha\langle \nabla f(x_n), g(\theta, x_n) \rangle + \frac{\alpha^2 L}{2}\|g(\theta, x_n)\|^2 \end{align*}

Taking expectation and using the two assumptions:

\[ \mathbb{E}_\theta[f(x_{n+1})] \leq \mathbb{E}_\theta[f(x_n)] - \alpha\|\nabla f(x_n)\|^2 + \frac{\alpha^2 LB}{2} \]

Summing over \( T \) iterations:

\[ f^\star \leq \mathbb{E}_\theta[f(x_T)] \leq f(x_0) - \alpha\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\|\nabla f(x_t)\|^2 + \frac{\alpha^2 LBT}{2} \]

Rearranging gives the result.

□

With a constant step-size, this gives convergence up to a ball of radius \( \alpha LB/2 \). With more assumptions (such as convexity) and suitable step-size strategies, one can establish global convergence for SGD.

SGD with momentum. Suppose \( f \) satisfies the expectation and bounded variance assumptions. We use the same technique as in the GD with momentum analysis, with the second sequence \( z_k = x_k + \dfrac{\beta}{1-\beta}(x_k - x_{k-1}) \), so: \[ z_{k+1} = z_k - \frac{\alpha}{1-\beta}g(\theta, x_k) \] Following the same steps and taking expectations: \begin{align*} \mathbb{E}[r_{k+1}^2] &\leq \mathbb{E}[r_k^2] - \frac{2\alpha}{1-\beta}\mathbb{E}[f(x_k) - f^\star] - \frac{2\alpha\beta}{(1-\beta)^2}\mathbb{E}[f(x_k) - f(x_{k-1})] + \frac{\alpha^2 B}{(1-\beta)^2} \end{align*} Summing over \( T \) iterations: \begin{align*} \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{i=0}^{T-1}(f(x_k) - f^\star)\right] \leq \frac{(1-\beta)\|x_0 - x^\star\|^2}{2\alpha} + \frac{\beta}{1-\beta}(f(x_0) - f^\star) + \frac{TB\alpha}{2(1-\beta)} \end{align*} Using convexity, the convergence for the weighted average of iterates is: \[ \mathbb{E}[f(\bar{x}_T) - f^\star] \leq \frac{(1-\beta)\|x_0 - x^\star\|^2}{2\alpha T} + \frac{\beta}{T(1-\beta)}(f(x_0) - f^\star) + \frac{B\alpha}{2(1-\beta)} \] With a constant step-size, this gives convergence up to a ball. A cleverly adaptive step-size strategy can improve the convergence further, but I will reserve this for another post.

Conclusion

Together, we have seen momentum from the perspective of a multi-step method for solving an equation. Furthermore, we have carried out convergence analyses of momentum in optimization, specifically in the \( L \)-smooth and convex setting. The situation will likely change in other regimes, especially in the non-convex setting where most problems in deep learning arise. However, I do not feel fully satisfied ending this post here, since there are many interesting stories around accelerated methods that I could not cover — such as Nesterov acceleration or averaging techniques.
