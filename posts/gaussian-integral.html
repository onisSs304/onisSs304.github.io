The Gaussian Integral

Contents

  2. Theorem
  3. Proof

Introduction

The Gaussian integral is one of the most beautiful results in analysis. It connects the exponential function \( e^{-x^2} \) — which has no elementary antiderivative — to the constant \( \sqrt{\pi} \), bridging real analysis and geometry.

It appears everywhere: probability theory (the normal distribution), physics (quantum mechanics, statistical mechanics), and signal processing.

Theorem
Theorem. The Gaussian integral satisfies: \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} \, dx = \sqrt{\pi} \]
Proof

Let \( I = \displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} \, dx \). We compute \( I^2 \) by writing:

\[ I^2 = \left( \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} \, dx \right) \left( \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-y^2} \, dy \right) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-(x^2 + y^2)} \, dx \, dy \]

Switching to polar coordinates with \( x = r\cos\theta \), \( y = r\sin\theta \), and \( dx\,dy = r\,dr\,d\theta \):

\[ I^2 = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\infty} e^{-r^2} \, r \, dr \, d\theta \]

The angular part gives a factor of \( 2\pi \). For the radial part, substituting \( u = r^2 \):

\[ \int_0^{\infty} e^{-r^2} r \, dr = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^{\infty} e^{-u} \, du = \frac{1}{2} \]

Therefore:

\[ I^2 = 2\pi \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \pi \]

Since \( I > 0 \), we conclude \( I = \sqrt{\pi} \).

□
Remarks

A useful generalization: for any \( a > 0 \), \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-ax^2} \, dx = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}} \] which follows by the substitution \( u = \sqrt{a}\, x \).

This directly gives the normalization of the Gaussian (normal) distribution. If \( X \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2) \), then its density integrates to 1 because: \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{x^2}{2\sigma^2}} dx = 1 \]
